# Epstein Was Not a Financial Manager — What SDNY's Own Memo Says About the Money

*Every quote in this document is extracted verbatim from OCR text of EFTA documents. Every EFTA page number has been verified against the original PDF using tools/find_correct_efta.py. The full evidence extraction is in tools/analysis_outputs/data/financial_evidence.txt.*

## The Core Finding

On EFTA page 02731148, in an internal prosecution memorandum prepared by the Southern District of New York, SDNY states:

> "We have not identified any accounts that are consistent with Epstein handling or investing other people's money." (Source: EFTA02731148)

This is one sentence in a dense paragraph about Epstein's banking. It has received almost no public attention. It should have, because it dismantles the foundational narrative about who Jeffrey Epstein was.

## What the Memo Documents

### 1. The Bank Review

SDNY, together with the FBI, reviewed financial records from all three banks that held Epstein-related accounts. The memo describes the scope:

> "Together with the FBI, we have reviewed a number of financial documents regarding Epstein's, Maxwell's, [redacted] finances. We have also been in touch for counsel representing JP Morgan, Deutsche Bank, and TD Bank, each of which have held accounts related to Epstein or his associates." (Source: EFTA02731148)

The banking timeline was straightforward:

> "Epstein held the bulk of his finances at JP Morgan from the early 2000s until 2013, when he opened multiple accounts at Deutsche Bank and transferred his funds from JP Morgan to Deutsche Bank. In 2019, Deutsche Bank closed Epstein's accounts, and his funds from those accounts were primarily transferred to the Bank of Puerto Rico." (Source: EFTA02731148)

What SDNY found in those accounts was consistent across all institutions:

> "Epstein's accounts appeared to be exclusively for the purpose of holding his own money. His Deutsche Bank accounts included checking accounts, money market accounts, and brokerage accounts for his charities, his consulting company Southern Trust Company, Inc., and several trusts, most of which appear to have been established to handle payments for Epstein's various expenses in connection with his properties and planes." (Source: EFTA02731148)

### 2. The Deutsche Bank Meeting

On September 12, 2019, SDNY met with Deutsche Bank's counsel for a detailed review:

> "On September 12, 2019, we met for several hours with counsel for Deutsche Bank, which held the majority of Epstein's finances from 2013 through 2019. Their presentation was extremely thorough, including a 50-page summary of their analysis. The presentation broke down Epstein's finances into a number of useful categories, including listing various types of transactions by type and recipient." (Source: EFTA02731148)

Deutsche Bank's conclusion:

> "At a high level, there was no immediate indication of financial criminal activity or misconduct, including based on our analysis and the internal investigation that continues to be in progress at Deutsche." (Source: EFTA02731148)

And specifically regarding client trading:

> "there do not appear to be any signs that Epstein was trading on behalf of any clients in Deutsche accounts during the period when he was a client, between 2013 and 2019." (Source: EFTA02731148)

### 3. TD Bank

TD Bank provided a separate data point:

> "in the fall of 2019, counsel for TD Bank reached out and informed us that several individuals and entities associated with Epstein held accounts at TD Bank, including accounts held by [redacted]. Epstein was not a signatory on any of the accounts, and TD Bank is in the process of closing all of the accounts." (Source: EFTA02731148)

### 4. Ongoing JP Morgan Review

At the time of the memo, the JP Morgan review was still in progress:

> "We are continuing to receive additional documents from JP Morgan, and the FBI is continuing to review financial records for any evidence of potential misconduct or potential obstruction" (Source: EFTA02731149)

## Where the Money Actually Came From

### The Wexner Proffer

On July 25, 2019, Wexner's attorneys provided a detailed account to SDNY. Their central claim:

> "over his years handling Wexner's finances, Epstein stole or otherwise misappropriated several hundred million dollars from Wexner. That misconduct, together with fees that Epstein paid himself for his services to Wexner, appears to account for virtually all of Epstein's wealth." (Source: EFTA02731146)

The mechanism was straightforward. Wexner gave Epstein control, and Epstein exploited it:

> "Wexner first met Epstein in the 1980s through a mutual friend who recommended Epstein as a financial adviser. After a couple other acquaintances recommended Epstein as well, Wexner brought Epstein on to help with his investments. Over time, Epstein became more involved in Wexner's finances until Epstein controlled all of Wexner's personal finances with virtually no oversight." (Source: EFTA02731146)

> "In 1991, Epstein obtained a power of attorney from Wexner, which enabled Epstein to handle the purchase and sale of real estate and other assets on Wexner's behalf." (Source: EFTA02731146)

### How Epstein Acquired Assets

> "At some point in 1998, Epstein sold himself the New York residence in which he resided until his arrest in 2019 at a deeply discounted price." (Source: EFTA02731146)

> "Epstein also sold himself a private plane that previously belonged to Wexner at a deeply discounted price." (Source: EFTA02731147)

A separate section of the memo (a forfeiture memorandum) provides additional detail on the New York residence:

> "In or about 1998, Wexner agreed to sell the New York Residence to Epstein for $20 million." (Source: EFTA02731070)

> "The New York Residence is valued at approximately $55-86 million" (Source: EFTA02731070)

The forfeiture memo also quantifies the theft:

> "Epstein stole or otherwise misappropriated more than $100 million from Wexner, in addition to receiving other fees in the tens or hundreds of millions of dollars during the duration of their decades-long financial relationship." (Source: EFTA02731070)

### The "Other Clients" Claim

There is a contradiction in the record. Wexner's attorneys told SDNY:

> "In the 1990s, Epstein told Wexner that he had other clients and that Wexner did not need to pay Epstein because Epstein was having fun and they would settle up later. Epstein introduced Wexner to a number of other people as his clients, and Wexner interacted with other prominent people who knew Epstein, such as Prince Andrew, professors at Harvard, and a sultan in Dubai." (Source: EFTA02731147)

But SDNY found no banking evidence of these other clients. Deutsche Bank found no client trading. JP Morgan had no client accounts. No bank held accounts consistent with managing other people's money.

Either the "other clients" were a fabrication by Epstein to explain his wealth to Wexner, or the relationships operated outside formal banking channels. The memo does not resolve which explanation is correct.

### The 2007 Discovery

The Wexner relationship ended when the theft was discovered:

> "Epstein handled Wexner's personal business affairs until 2007, when Wexner learned that Epstein had stolen or otherwise misappropriated several hundred million dollars from Wexner." (Source: EFTA02731147)

Epstein's explanation to Wexner at the time is notable:

> "Epstein told Wexner that he was having legal problems involving an overly aggressive police chief and some sort of massage. Epstein said he was being blackmailed and it could get messy, so it would be best for Wexner's wife to take over the family finances." (Source: EFTA02731147)

Wexner's wife then discovered the extent:

> "Wexner's wife looked into the state of their finances and discovered that Epstein had misappropriated a significant amount of the family's funds. For example, Epstein frequently bought property on behalf of the Wexners and then sold it to himself for a fraction of the cost." (Source: EFTA02731147)

The resolution was private:

> "The Wexners did not want to bring unnecessary public attention to the issue, so they withdrew the power of attorney, and hired counsel to negotiate a private settlement with Epstein. As a result of those negotiations, Epstein agreed to return $100 million to the Wexners, which he did in January of 2008." (Source: EFTA02731147)

### Leon Black — Context

Separately, the memo documents payments from Leon Black:

> "payments for work performed over the period 2012 through 2017 totaled $158 million." (Source: EFTA02730998)

These payments went to Southern Trust Company, Inc. — the same entity that appeared in Epstein's Deutsche Bank accounts. This is consistent with the "no client accounts" finding: Black paid Epstein's company for services. The money flowed into Epstein's own accounts, not into accounts where Epstein managed Black's money.

> "Epstein and Black negotiated a written service agreement [...] entered into between Black and Southern Trust Company, Inc., an Epstein affiliate" (Source: EFTA02731011)

> "Epstein represented to Black that he generally charged clients $40 million per year for his advice" (Source: EFTA02731011)

## The Cameras: Same Page, Same Conclusion

The no-cameras finding appears on the same EFTA page as the no-client-accounts finding (EFTA02731148). Both appear in Section 2 and Section 3 of the same memorandum, within the same review of physical and financial evidence gathered after Epstein's arrest.

> "contrary to some news reports, these searches did not reveal any cameras in any of the bedrooms or massage rooms." (Source: EFTA02731148)

Victims believed cameras existed. One stated:

> "[redacted] further claimed that the FBI told her that Epstein had cameras watching her at all times, including while in the bathroom (we are not aware of any such cameras)." (Source: EFTA02731142)

The memo records SDNY's assessment parenthetically: "we are not aware of any such cameras." Combined with the search warrant finding of no cameras in bedrooms or massage rooms, the surveillance-for-blackmail theory is not supported by the camera search, but is complicated by the extensive photographic material and DVR systems the FBI did seize.

## What This Means

The popular narrative about Epstein involves two key claims:

1. **He was a hedge fund manager or financial adviser** with a client list of wealthy and powerful people.
2. **He used hidden cameras** to record compromising activity and leverage it for protection or profit.

SDNY's own prosecution memo, written for internal use and not for public consumption, finds no evidence supporting the first claim. The accounts were personal. On the second: no cameras were found installed in bedrooms or massage rooms at the time of the search. However, DVR surveillance systems, CDs with handwritten labels including names, and hundreds of photographs were seized.

What SDNY did find was simpler: Epstein stole from one very wealthy person over two decades, accumulated hundreds of millions through theft and fees, and used that money to fund his own lifestyle, properties, and planes. When discovered, the victim settled privately and did not press charges.

## What This Does NOT Tell Us

This analysis has significant limitations:

1. **The Wexner proffer came from Wexner's attorneys.** SDNY recorded what they were told. This is an attorney proffer, not sworn testimony. Wexner's lawyers had an incentive to characterize the relationship as one-directional exploitation.

2. **"No immediate indication" is not "no evidence."** Deutsche Bank's phrase is cautious. Their internal investigation was still in progress at the time.

3. **The JP Morgan review was incomplete.** The memo notes they were "continuing to receive additional documents from JP Morgan." Whatever those documents contained is not reflected in this memo.

4. **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.** SDNY did not find client accounts. This does not prove client accounts never existed at other institutions, in other jurisdictions, or through other mechanisms.

5. **The memo says nothing about offshore accounts.** The Virgin Islands banking is not discussed. Neither are any accounts in other countries.

6. **The $100 million settlement may not represent the full amount stolen.** The forfeiture memo says "more than $100 million" was misappropriated, and Epstein returned exactly $100 million. The full amount taken is unclear.

7. **The Leon Black payments ($158M) arrived after the Wexner relationship ended.** The "virtually all" assessment may not account for post-2007 income.

8. **This is a single memo.** Other EFTA documents may contain additional financial information that modifies or contradicts these findings.
